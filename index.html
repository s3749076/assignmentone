Project

Overview

Upon the conclusion of the 2019 Australian Federal Election I realised a large problem which an IT solution would be most appropriate. During the election cycle candidates from every constituency across the country make claims about their opposition’s plans for government and how their alternative is scientifically and objectively better. From the infinite number of claims and attempts at slandering an oppositions crediantals, comes an impossible contradiction in that how can so many parties and candidates all be right and how can so many polarizing policies addressing the same issue all be perfect? The solution I would put forward is that of a mobile application which would break down the election down to a few key issues, present the policy agenda of differing major parties, then what economists or other experts estimate as being the most positive action to take.

Motivation

The motivation for this project is that I have always been heavily interested in the political processes of Australia, not for the furthering of any particular dogma or doctrine but rather an appreciation of the democracy we live in and the desire to make the people’s voting right as powerful as possible. I want everyone to vote the way that will benefit them and their family rather than being manipulated by lies and deception spread during the heated election campaigns. I believe if voters had access to the breakdown of individual policy and the opinion of experts they would know which party truly holds the values they care about and would empower the voters to have the government in power that will truly meet their needs, whilst forcing rejected parties to adapt their policies. The audience of this app would be the 16.5 million Australians [4] who are eligible to vote, even if only a few people realise what party actually represents their interests through the project it would be worthwhile.

Description

The main features of the app can be defined into three separate sections: The selection of the most popular political parties, the election issues and the policy put forward by the party selected, and how experts assess the policy in regard to effectiveness in addressing the problem.

Part Selection

The party selector element of the app would be the initial step when opening the app. The user will be presented with the icon of the party accompanied with the name, leader’s name, deputy leader’s name, and the name of the candidate in their electorate if that information is provided and if such a candidate is running in said electorate. To continue the use of the app the user would have to select a party to further investigate. An example of such a lay out would look like the following.

As demonstrated, the user would see the parties logo above its name, leader, deputy and candidate for that area. The area I based the results from is the electorate of Gorton in Victoria’s western suburbs. An example of what an empty candidacy would look like can be seen in the Nationals icon where the candidate is listed as ‘none’ due to the fact that the nationals did not contest the seat of Gorton during the 2019 Federal Election. The parties that will be displayed within this segment of the app are those identified as being major/minor parties by the Parliament of Australia that are still currently active. The list of parties which will be present are: the Australian Labor Party, the Liberal Party of Australia, The National Party of Australia, One Nation, Australian Greens, Katter’s Australian Party, Palmer United Party, and the Centre Alliance [5]

Election Issues

This segment of the app succeeds the party selection screen, at this point the user would follow up the selection of a party with an issue to view that parties policy and the expert analysis. The topics that will be included in this segment of the app would be determined based on the constraints in developing that project, if enough time was given then many more issues would be included. The top issues of the 2019 election, as denoted by the data ABC news published from their Vote Compass project [6], are in descending importance to voters: Environment, Economy, Healthcare, Superannuation and pensions, Employment, Immigration and refugees, Government accountability, Education, Poverty and inequality, Housing and cost of living, LGBTQ rights, Energy, Security, Infrastructure, Freedom of speech/religion, Gender equality, Indigenous issues, and Industrial relations. In a prototype or alpha state the app would feature the first 6 topics as they are the most important to voters and would reduce the workload to a manageable amount. From each of the topics featured the policy of each of the parties listed in the previous section would be provided. Each topic may also be broken down into more specific issues or the policy section would feature numerous policy ideas. Using Environment as an example we would list the party policy for each of the major and minor parties. Here is an example of what would be displayed (ignoring the lack of formating or visual style)

As demonstrated above, depending on what party was selected (column left), then what policy area is selected (column middle) information on what policy said party provides is displayed. The distinction here is that the information to be included should be kept to only physical goals or funding not promises or estimations of what certain unrelated policies would do to impact the environment. It is critical that the importation of these policies within the app is done carefully as propaganda featured within policy outlines cannot be included as it would completely undermine the purpose of the app. The above example shows the 3 different outputs possible when a user checks out the policy for any party. The Liberal and Labor party both present their very polar funding outlines, alternatively the National Party refers back to the Liberal policy as their own (as they are a Coalition). The final and least likely output is that the party doesn’t provide any policy for that topic, as demonstrated by the United Australia Party who provided no environmental policy for the 2019 election.

Expert Assessment

This final segment of the app would feature an expert opinion of the policy featured in the previous page. This opinion would have to be a critical evaluation of the policy idea and provide an estimation on the effect such a policy would have (if the effect isn’t what the party claims it would be). An example of this is the Climate Council’s criticism of the incumbent Liberal/National government’s Emissions Reduction Fund, saying that “the Federal Government’s climate performance over the past five-years as the defining leadership failure of the past decade” [11] while also providing a graph demonstrating that the Carbon Taxation period demonstrated a decrease in emissions while the Emissions Reduction Fund period say an increase in emissions.

As demonstrated this segment would have an expert opinion and evidence demonstrating that their policy is either ineffectual in addressing the issue or has no effect. This example shows the Climate Council claiming that the government’s policy direction will not address the issue of carbon emissions whilst providing that the Carbon Tax policy established by the Gillard government had real reductions on carbon emissions.

A computer device would be used to develop/program the app whilst the phone would be used to properly demo test the app.

Skills Required

The skills required would be the ability to use either, python, java, MIT App inventor, or another app developing software tool. More specifically the application of an understanding of the design conventions that need to be implemented into an app which would be hopefully used by those of varying age and education. One of the most critical skills would be the inclusion of accessibility concerns involving the vast variety of potential users, from aged users, to non-enlgish speakers to visually handicapped people a large number of particular of differing disadvantages or problems potential users would have in accessing and appropriately understanding the app and the information it provides.

Outcome

If the project was successful then the outcome would be very difficult to measure without direct feedback from users as the anonymous nature of voting means there would be no metric of which voters changed voting habits to measure. The most desirable outcome of the project is not to change people’s vote or to influence certain parties into power but rather to empower voters to fully understand the direction the country will take depending on who they cast their votes for. Ultimately the better informed the voting population is the better life will be for every Australian.
